How to Become a Software Developer

March 6th, 2023 | #work #software #tutorial

So you want to become a software developer?

Makes sense, a lot of people see software dev as their ticket to a 6-figure salary. But it’s not as simple as taking a class or 2 and getting a junior role at Google. The field is tough to get a grasp of. Even people in-field like yours truly have a hard time seeing the forest for the trees. Let's dive into some basics.

“Can I learn to code?”

Anton Ego puts it best: “Not everyone can become a great artist, but a great artist can come from anywhere.” You don’t have to be young, smart, or talented to get into coding. It’s more of a mindset and general interest in the field.

If you can’t sit down at a computer for 4 hours and not get horribly depressed, or if encountering roadblocks upsets you, do us all a favor and avoid coding. Know thyself, no salary is worth your mental wellbeing.

“Don’t you need a college degree to even be considered for employment?”

Not necessarily! I’m not going to blow hot air up your butt and tell you it's not a huge boon to have a degree on your resume, but recently it's becoming less important. Big companies like Meta, Google, and Amazon care more about what you know. My recommendation is that if you are college-bound anyway or just starting your degree to consider getting a CS major.

It's important to note that what they teach you in college is NOT what businesses will expect from you. College teaches you the theory behind computer logic, how we have improved the underlying tech over time, and if you’re lucky some modern approaches to applying these ideas. Academia CS is aimed at making ‘idea guys’ that understand CS as a field of research. For the most part, businesses will be looking to hire workers who know the tools. Of course there is overlap between the two, but a lot of your time will be spent on ideas and not head-down practice. In the long run having this deep theoretical knowledge will help, but it's not necessary to get a rewarding job or work on meaningful projects.

“What about code camps/self-taught?”

Code camps are often scams. The first thing you should be asking a code camp recruiter is the % placement in jobs immediately after graduating. Anything other than an enthusiastic answer is a red flag. Definitely consider this option as an adult learner, but use good judgment before signing up anywhere.

Self-taught coworkers of mine are incredibly good at what they do. My guess as to why is that they have lots of willpower. I tacitly recommend this route because you have to be pretty big-brained to make it on your own. However, if you are the type of person that can motivate themselves this is a rewarding route.

“How do I get started today?”

Some people recommend starting with CodeAcademy. I feel that it's a good hour-long test to see if you have the basic logic and desire to be a programmer.

Past that I would STRONGLY recommend looking over https://roadmap.sh/ and researching each path and what they do. Think of some projects you would like to do and dive into one of these learning paths. Bonus points if you upload proof-of-concepts, demos, and investigative projects for any of the topics listed.

If you would like to get into the IT side of things, I would recommend looking over https://i.redd.it/lni6tgghq0z21.jpg as a general certificate guide as you explore through that side of tech. Certifications are always great, and as a software dev having awareness of IT can certainly set you apart.

And of course, the most important thing is to stay interested. No one learns well by choking down information. Find the fun and the learning will come naturally.
