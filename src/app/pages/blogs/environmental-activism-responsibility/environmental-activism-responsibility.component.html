Most of us know about the looming threat of climate change. We are already past the point of no return for rising global temperatures. We have got to do something to mitigate climate disaster, but what? Donate $100 to team trees because of Mr. Beast says so? Stop eating bacon? Bike 20 miles to work? What are individuals actually called to do? What choices are actually goal-oriented?

First, some assumptions:

  • People are bound by Rational Choice Theory - that they make choices aligned with their own self interests.
  • Therefore, solutions should not rely on goodwill
  • Meaning that preventative measures ought to be authoritative and have consequences

So why bring this up? I don't trust corporations, charities, or 'people' to do the right thing. I don't trust corporations to ensure my food is safe to consume. I don't trust charities to make real change and not serve corporate interests. I don't trust people to drive sober. What mitigates all of this is good policy.

I can hear the token libertarian in the back screaming "What about Corporate Social Responsibility? Companies will be climate conscious because its profitable marketing!" Sure, many companies are making a good effort to be climate conscious. However, corporations are only 'conscious' to the point where its cost-effective. The fact is that 'solutions' like Carbon Offsetting are a band-aid solution to a rapidly deteriorating situation. But don't take my word for it, here is an excerpt from an e-mail conversation with Sandy Smith-Nonini professor of Anthropology and Environmental Justice.

Unfortunately, corporate lawyers and Wall St. have had about 20 years now to study up on how to continue growth as usual, and profit while doing cosmetic “greening” – so greenwashing is rampant, and Corp. Social. Resp. (CSR) or Environmental and Social Governance (ESG) is more or less voluntary – and up to an investor to investigate. Which means you need to learn more about carbon trading, and offsets before sending your money off (...) In short, it all depends on enforcing a C-price and lowering the C-price over time to work in a C market – and the EU got all this wrong in their initial efforts and had to start over again, and still it is not really enforced. Without international agreements that penalize polluters, it can’t work. It is like a game –(the EU C-market was heavily shaped by corporate lobbying); Biggest problem is monitoring offsets on a global level – (would take an army of monitors and actual fear of enforcement by companies), and very large companies will just pay to pollute, and not change their practices if the C price is not set high enough. To date C markets have become another form of financialization – new green derivatives that are opaque in their complexity, and perfect for profiteering. The most common type, forest management, is not working because planting new trees does not a carbon sink make (think rain forest or mature, climax forest). It takes literally hundreds of years for forests to mature, and trees to draw C down, and we don’t have that time.

The point I am making is that making Marginal, Altruistic choices to prevent individual carbon emission are not efficient or scalable. It's just not a good strategy to assume that actors will suddenly ascend past the tragedy of the commons. Ok, so if carbon offsetting isn't the min-max strategy we are looking for, what is? My opinion lies closely to American educator and environmentalist Bill McKibben. The truth is that the wellspring of change lies with out policy makers. See below for an excerpt from "Conservation vs. Activism".

Say you have a certain amount of time and money with which to make change-call it x, since that is what we mathematicians call things. The trick is to increase that x by multiplication, not addition.The trick is to take that 5 percent of people who really care and make them count for far more than 5 percent. And the trick to that is democracy.

If people who care about climate change mobilize politically, 5 percent will be more than enough too-it will persuade senators, congressmen, and presidents to back strict legislation that will set real caps on emissions and fund real research on the technologies we need. If such laws pass, they would change the behavior of 95 percent of Americans, including reluctant in-laws. This kind of equation isn't hypothetical. Two years ago, I helped organize a march across Vermont that called on our leaders to work for deep cuts in carbon emissions. A thousand of us walked the sixty-mile route-one Vermonter in six hundred. And yet that was enough to get all of our legislators, including the conservative Republicans, to sign on to our pledge.

Now just because activism is a priority doesn't mean we should just stop caring about conservation. Rather, given that people have only so many 'spoons' to spend on given tasks, we ought to consider better ways to spend our 'spoons' for longitudinal change.

Here is a priority list heuristic that I think makes sense:

  • Get to where you have Capacity to Contribute: double check that you have the ability to contribute without putting yourself at risk.
  • Educate Yourself: Corporations are hard at work misleading the public so they keep profits up. Don't let them fool you.
  • Get Politically Involved: Join an initiative, NGO, or volunteer group. Vote. Write to your congressperson. Policy is the wellspring of change.
  • Lifestyle Changes: Now consider what changes you can make in your own life. Vegan Mondays? Taking one less international vacation? Air Drying your clothes? Good stuff.
  • Donate: Only donate after you have considered all the above items. Money talks, but often feet-on-the-ground protests speak louder.

So in-summary: know your stance, be secure in the facts, and shoot for real policy change with activism. Then, consider what conservation actions make sense for you and your life.

Screw in a new light bulb? Sure. Screw in a new global treaty? Now we're talking.
~Bill McKibben
